An invitation to spatial epidemiology

I didn’t realize I was a spatial epidemiologist until I’d been doing it for 10 years, but you don’t have to wait that long.

Author

Jon Zelner

Published

March 20, 2023

Introduction

One theme that comes up consistently in teaching about and talking to students and others about spatial epidemiology is that it is defined by methodological sophistication and a light aura of inaccessibility. This makes sense: Making maps and measuring and modeling spatial relationships might seem as though it is outside of the classic analytic toolkit of working epidemiologists, not to mention other fields in public health, medicine, and the social sciences.

The diagram in Figure 1 reflects my interpretation of the way someone coming to this area of research and practice for the first time might think of it. In essence, spatial epidemiology or analysis is taken to be a set of relatively fixed and well-described ideas and procedures connected to a highly technical set of methods. If we hold this view, commencing a spatial project requires us to embark with a backpack already filled with specialized spatial tools.

You are probably a spatial analyst already

In this essay, my goal is to chip away a bit at this idea and to present spatial epidemiology - and in public health more generally - as an emergent phenomenon that acts more as a wrapper around a core set of ideas and tools that are part of the working arsenal of most professionals, students and researchers in public health.

I’ll do this both through review of the key concepts at the center of spatial epidemiology and their connection to other key concepts, but also through a bit of autobiography about my own intellectual and professional trajectory.

Maps: Gateway or destination?

A quick bit of personal history may be instructive here. I began working as an epidemiologist or epidemology-adjacent type of person some time around 2006. For my dissertation research at the University of Michigan, I worked on a project in which we examined the role of social networks as sources of risk and protection against diarrheal disease and other infections in a group of villages in an area of rural Ecuador:

Looking back, this is indisputably a spatial analysis: We were interested in how local social contexts impacted variation in health outcomes across a set of 20+ villages and also how within-village variation in social connectivity impacted risk within villages. In this analysis, we employed multi-level data about common characteristics of individual villages, households, and the individuals within them. But at this time, I thought of myself as doing a few things, but none of them were spatial:

  1. Infectious disease epidemiology: Why and how do people become infected with various pathogens?
  2. Social epidemiology: How do social relationships impact disease outcomes?
  3. Network analysis: How does the structure of relationships impact individual and community health?

As it happened, all of these things were correct, but what I didn’t really understand at the time was how the collection of these different approaches into one analysis was what made it spatial or geographic in nature.
