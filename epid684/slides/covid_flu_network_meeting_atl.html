Rethinking social causes, effects, and interventions in epidemiological systems

A very unpleasant thought experiment

  • In January 2024, a new viral respiratory pathogen for which there are no effective vaccines or therapeutics emerges.

  • Age-specific case-fatality rates are as-yet unknown.

  • It is unclear which non-pharmaceutical interventions will be effective for preventing transmission.

  • How to best manage infection in an acute-care setting is an open question.

  • Given these starting conditions, would you expect to see reduced inequity in infection and death as compared to COVID-19?

Structural determinants of unequal risk remain firmly in place

  • Occupational and residential segregation

  • Income and wealth inequality

  • Structural racism in public health, social policy, and medical practice

  • And too many other mechanisms to enumerate them all‚Ä¶

How much progress can we make by enumerating and attacking the mid-stream drivers of infection inequity?

Flow diagram of connections between high-level, structural causes, intervening mechanisms, and downstream exposure risks, from (2)

Fundamental cause theory (FCT) gives some insight into why this may often lead to a dead end.

Theoretical diagram from (3) showing relationships between racism, socioeconomic inequity and racial inequity in disease outcomes.

Fundamental causes are characterized by their pervasiveness and durability.

  1. A fundamental cause is connected to inequity in outcomes through numerous intermediary mechanisms (4).

  2. A fundamental cause drive inequity across multiple, diverse health outcomes.

  3. New technologies cause may inequities to persist or grow if they diffuse through the population along existing socioeconomic structures.

A helpful example

Epidemiologists and policymakers are used to thinking about well-defined interventions.

‚ÄòWell-defined‚Äô interventions follow SUTVA principles (6):

  1. Modalities of intervention are as explicit as possible.

  2. Direct vs. indirect effects of treatment are clearly identified.

  3. Quantitative relationships between intervention and outcome are replicable across subjects and contexts.

Well-defined interventions are essential tools of effective public health and clinical medicine.

But are there well-defined interventions that can address the fundamental causes of infection inequity?

The only ‚Äòwell-defined intervention‚Äô for racial disparities that can attack all of the pathways connecting structural racism to health inequity (as described by Richardson (8), with a healthy dose of irony)

Why might well-defined interventions be limited tools for attacking the structural determinants of inequity?

  • Downstream factors addressed by well-defined interventions may be chasing a moving target (6,9).

  • Causal inference targeting the ‚Äòclosest possible world‚Äô (10) biases towards conservative interventions.

  • An intervention framework assuming a tight connection between policy(makers) and discrete interventions may not be an appropriate frame for problems requiring collective action. (6,11)

What should models and modelers do, then?

  • Schwartz et al. (6) argue for examination of causal mediators to help understand an overarching theoretical explanation rather than to highlight individual mechanisms of intervention.

  • Accept greater uncertainty about causal links between insights, discrete actions, and disease outcomes.

  • This is inferior to a proximal approach iff more proximal approaches can actually make a meaningful dent in infection inequity.

  • The evidence suggests‚Ä¶otherwise.

Does this mean we have to fix every social problem to have an impact?

  • FCT provides insight into what puts poeple ‚Äúat risk of risk‚Äù and how these relationships may change over time.

  • If our outcomes of interest are overdetermined by structural inequities, purely downstream efforts are doomed to fail and may be counterproductive.

  • FCT forces us to consider the implications of the dynamic relationships between social systems and health outcomes.
